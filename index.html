OSMOS
Science mode

This is not what it objectively looks like.

OSMOS is an artistic interpretation, not a measurement. It's inspired by documented cases of olfactory‑visual synesthesia — a rare condition where a smell involuntarily triggers a consistent visual experience: colour, shape, motion, even texture, with no learned or logical connection to the object itself. That's different from ordinary cross‑modal association, the common, culturally shared sense that lemons "feel" yellow or mint "feels" green.

Every scent below is broken into nine qualities researchers use to describe odour character. Those numbers drive the colour, motion and geometry you see — sweetness softens particles into orbs, bitterness and earthiness sharpen them into crystalline fragments, florals and smoke pull them into long drifting ribbons. No two synesthetes report identical mappings, so treat this as one possible signal, not the truth.

When the camera is on, OSMOS tries to recognise everyday objects it can see and draws a box around anything it can also assign a scent profile to. That's ordinary object recognition, not smell detection — the camera can tell a banana from a book, not what either actually smells like right now. Tap a box to load that object's profile.

the hidden visual language of smell

Some people never merely remember a colour when they smell coffee — they see one, involuntarily, every time. OSMOS borrows that rare wiring. Name any scent and watch a living signal bloom: not a picture of the thing, but of the feeling reported by minds that see smell.

No camera data leaves your device. If you enable the camera, optional object recognition also runs locally in your browser — nothing is uploaded anywhere.
